Special Needs

General Overview

A person with special needs, or a person with disability (PWD) is one with any of various difficulties, such as physical, emotional, behavioral, or learning disabilities or impairments, that causes an individual to require additional or specialized services or accommodations, such as in education or recreation. Special Needs

General Overview

A person with special needs, or a person with disability (PWD) is one with any of various difficulties, such as physical, emotional, behavioral, or learning disabilities or impairments, that causes an individual to require additional or specialized services or accommodations, such as in education or recreation. Approximately 3% of the Singaporean population has some form of disabilities. Persons with sensory (hearing or visual impairments) and physical disabilities constitute half of the disability group, the other half is made up with those with autism and intellectual disabilities. 4,000 children were diagnosed with special developmental needs every year in 2015 and this number is likely to go up.

When discussing the issue of special needs, PWDs are not the only ones impacted. Their caregivers are affected as well. We will explore these 2 groups in this study guide.

Below, we will be discussing 3 of the key issues faced by PWDs.

Key Issues

Attitudinal Barriers

Some may have preconceived misconceptions about PWDs, be it about their capabilities or behaviour.

Stereotyping and Stigma

People may have stereotypes against PWDs, assuming their quality of life is poor, or that they are unhealthy because of their impairments. With false impressions of PWDs, many discriminate against PWDs, refusing to let them integrate into society. Some even believe that PWD’s impairment makes them an inferior “second-class citizen”.

PWDs are often dismissed as incapable of accomplishing simple tasks before they try. However, although they have disabilities, they are often able to complete the same tasks as us. Ignorance can also further reinforce stereotypes and prejudice against PWDs.

Difficulties in communicating with PWDs

People often feel sorry for PWDs, which tends to lead to patronising attitudes. However, this may make PWDs feel worse. This is because many PWDs generally are not looking for pity or charity, but rather equal opportunity to earn their own way and live independently.

Many fear that they will “say or do something wrong” around PWDs and hence avoid PWDs. They therefore avert their own discomfort by avoiding the individual with a disability. This can cause PWDs to feel alienated from society and alone.

Denial

Many disabilities are “hidden”. 74% of PWDs don’t use a wheelchair or anything that visually signals their impairment. Examples include learning disabilities, psychiatric disabilities, epilepsy and heart conditions. People can tend to believe these are not bona fide disabilities needing accommodation, thus depriving such PWDs of much needed accommodation.

They may not believe that PWDs genuinely need help or fail to spot the difficulties faced because of this denial. PWDs may also be reluctant to openly disclose information about their disability in fear that their peers’ perception of them will change due to the stereotypes surrounding their condition.

What are groups that raise awareness about such stereotypes?

Disabled People’s Association (DPA) Singapore

The DPA advocates on behalf of and with PWDs to engage with stakeholders and, policy, aiming to create awareness about as well as engage people in discussions about disability.

SPD -- UNLABEL campaign

SPD is a Singapore charity aiming to help PWDs maximise their potential and integrate into society.

Participants are encouraged to do research on these organisations and their efforts to raise awareness.

Employment Opportunities

People with disabilities are far less likely to be employed. Singapore holds one of the lowest disability employment rates amongst developed cities in the world with only 4.9% of Persons With Disabilities (PWDs) employed in the workforce, while those without disabilities had a 76.5% employment rate. This means that only 5 out of every 100 PWDs are participating in the workforce and we are missing out on a potential workforce of 160, 000 people.

Workplace Discrimination: Direct Discrimination

PWDs are often treated less favourably than others.

Disrespect and Personal Attacks

PWDs can be on the receiving end of uncivil language and uncouth behaviour in the workplace where coworkers may make jokes about their disability. Colleagues may also easily lose their patience, or make degrading remarks.

Unequal remuneration, recognition and training opportunities¹

PWDs may be paid less for doing the same work as, or sometimes doing even more work than their non-disabled peers. PWDs may be denied training opportunities awarded to all employees, and promotions may be offered to other less qualified employees who are not PWDs

Social Exclusion

There may be communication barriers between PWDs and their coworkers resulting in social exclusion of PWDs, such as them being ignored when they ask for clarifications, or alienation where PWDs are not included in social gatherings

Workplace Discrimination: Facilities and accommodation

Employers might not understand the need for job redesign so some provide less than ideal accommodations. Some employers hire people with disabilities without fully preparing to accommodate them, thinking that routine ways of doing things will work. However, lack of accommodations leaves PWDs unable to work effectively, or at all. Yet, such accommodations are often inexpensive or easy to implement.

Workplace Discrimination: Indirect Discrimination

There are several examples of indirect discrimination. For instance, colleagues and employers indirectly discriminate when they fail to respond to incidents of discrimination.

This may also involve paternalistic or patronising attitudes. Coworkers put on a veneer of benevolence while interacting with PWDs, and may treat PWDs as if they are incapable of making good judgements or decisions. Coworkers or superiors may refuse to assign certain tasks they deem PWDs incapable of executing without letting them try or asking their opinion

Colleagues and supervisors may not be as understanding towards the limitations of PWDs’ disabilities, and may not accommodate their needs effectively.

Consequences

Feelings of alienation can negatively impact PWD’s mental health, while those with chronic mental illnesses can suffer a deteriorating condition. Many PWDs who have experienced workplace discrimination reported feeling “worthless”, and start doubting themself because they feel that no matter what they do, it is not good enough to be respected as and treated as their non-disabled counterparts.

As a result of unpleasant working experiences, PWDs may start to be distrustful of future places of employment, and instead prefer to work alone. This may exacerbate their social alienation, and above negative implications.

What are grants that support employers that hire differently-abled individuals?

The Open Door Programme (ODP) Grant is a government funded initiative, the programme aims to encourage employers to hire, train and integrate persons with disabilities. The Job Redesign Grant supports redesigning of jobs to help employers recruit and retain PWDs by providing an inclusive and workable workplace for PWDs. The SG Enable Training Grant supports skills-upgrading of PWDs, and training of employees with disabilities.

What are some social enterprises that employ PWDs?

Dining in the Dark

This ‘Dining in the Dark’ experience is organised by the Singapore Association of the Visually Handicapped to raise public awareness of the challenges that persons with vision impairment face.

Liho

Liho is aiming for stores to employ and be managed by PWDs, partnering with Singapore Association for the Deaf to provide appropriate training to PWDs.

National University Hospital (NUH)

NUH engaged potential intellectually challenged students for internships in various departments. These eventually became their full-time jobs.

Do find out more about these enterprises and their initiatives.

Caregiver Burnout

The difficulties faced by PWDs also cause ripple effects in their communities, in the form of the troubles and obstacles that their caregivers face.

37% of caregivers in Singapore have been providing care for over a decade. Studies have revealed our caregivers face higher mental health risks. Among carers of stroke survivors, 40.2% have depressive symptoms. 4 in 10 caregivers reported feeling psychologically distressed, with 6 in 10 feeling burdened by caregiving. Family caregivers have been shown to be less likely to attend to their own health and self-care needs, negatively affecting their well-being.

Causes of Caregiver Burnout

The stress of caregiving accumulates over time. If left unchecked, physical, emotional and mental exhaustion can cause burnout. Caregivers often prioritise PWDs’ needs and interests, neglecting their own wellbeing. Caregivers also feel the discrimination faced by PWDs.

Having unrealistic expectations may contribute to caregivers’ stress. They may expect their loved one’s condition to improve from their care. However, this may not be possible for those with progressive conditions such as dementia.

Caregiver stress may also arise from lack of support or pressure and expectations from family. Sole caregivers may not have adequate support for them to take breaks. Unrealistic demands and expectations of other family members may add on to caregivers’ stress.

Many caregivers, such as working caregivers and the sandwich generation also have to manage multiple commitments and responsibilities.

Issues that Caregivers face

Anger and frustration

It can be distressing to put one's life on hold to care for someone else. The financial difficulties associated with caring for PWDs, and difficulties of care may also be frustrating.

Depression

Between 40% and 70% of family caregivers experience symptoms of depression. This can be due to lack of self care, isolation and loneliness faced by caregivers.

Physical strain

Caregivers may not have time to take care of themselves. This includes exercising, eating properly and getting sufficient rest.

Lack of Understanding of Caregivers within Community

Many do not understand caregivers’ sacrifices and their burnout. Caregivers may struggle with the loss of income as they leave their jobs or work fewer hours. Hence, some are rude when affected by PWDs. Caregivers also acutely feel discrimination faced by PWDs in their care.

Caregivers may require leave or flexible schedules. Caregiver discrimination is when employers deny caregiver employers leave or retaliate against them for taking leave. Employers often terminate employees because of their caregiver status, assuming caregivers are less competent or committed.

What are current solutions in place to support caregivers of PWDs?

Technical Support

Therapy may help caregivers better cope with isolation, grief and stress that they face. Respite care allows caregivers to take a break from caregiving, but can be costly. Many caregivers feel guilty for not attending to their loved one.

Community support

Caregivers of children with autism have set up initiatives such as NeuroDiverCity, a website which gathers and shares services and resources that parents can review.

‘Caregivers’ Space’

Caregivers' Space serves as a meeting place for peer support groups, training of caregivers of PWDs, and engagement sessions by Voluntary Welfare Organisations as well as community partners.

What are some organisations which work to support caregivers of PWDs?

Building an Inclusive Society

Initiatives to build a more inclusive society for PWDs include:

  1. The 2019 Revised Code on Accessibility, which strengthened requirements to address the needs of PWD for more disability-friendly buildings and facilities
  2. The Introduction of para sports in the Singapore National Games and gradual conversion of all ActiveSG gyms to inclusive gyms allowed for more inclusive sports, leisure, and recreation
  3. Making public transportation more accessible. For instance, all new public buses are fitted with digital Passenger Information Display System to enable persons with visual impairment and hearing loss to better access information, all public buses made wheelchair accessible, and provision of at least 1 barrier-free access route at all MRT and LRT stations and bus interchanges.
