Migrant Workers

General Overview

The official definition of “Migrant Workers” for this edition of SLS specifically refers to Singaporean migrant construction workers. This will apply to all discussions and information in this guide.

Last year as of December 2020, the Ministry of Manpower announced that there are around 311000 foreigners working in Singapore in the construction, marine and processing sectors. This is a decrease from 370000 foreigners residing in Singapore in December 2019.

Migrant workers to Singapore come from various countries, including Malaysia, Bangladesh, India, Indonesia and the Philippines.

Key Issues

Migrant Worker Rights

Protecting migrant worker rights is one of the toughest challenges for migrant workers in Singapore.

The first prevalent problem is that migrant workers are subject to labour right abuses and unfair treatment by employers.¹ Such labour right abuses can include restriction of movement and confiscation of passports. This means that migrant workers have to report to their superiors before they are able to go anywhere, which poses a high level of danger to the migrant workers if their superiors are not free in cases of emergencies. The restriction of freedom also violates the human rights of migrant workers. Abuse from employers can also come in the form of insufficient work injury payouts. When the migrant workers are injured, they cannot work and thus rely on payouts to sustain their livelihood. If there are insufficient payouts, the basic living standards of migrant workers could be at risk.

Secondly, the migrant workers face exploitation by employers rooted in an inherent power asymmetry between employers and employees. This can be caused by many factors, such as the fact that migrant workers owe a lot of money to employment agencies for bringing them from their home country to Singapore and getting them jobs. For context, some paid as much as $10000 in agent fees to work in Singapore, 20 times their paycheck. The huge debt is exacerbated by the fact that in order to pay for the high fees, many had to sell jewelry or land, borrow money from relatives, or take out bank loans, adding to the pressure on the worker to make money in Singapore. In addition, their pay can be very low, as low as $18 per day, or $540 per month for hard manual labour.² This low pay is a result of an absence of a minimum wage in Singapore. Yet, migrant workers frequently work very long hours, incentivised by their huge debt. The imbalance between the long hours that the workers work and their low pay points to exploitation of migrant workforce by employers, such as repatriation threats made, leading to a significant disadvantage for foreign workers during employer-employee negotiations.

There is, fortunately, a maximum working hour limit for “foreign workers working under contract with an employer” in Singapore. Work time is capped at 44 hours a week by law.³ Nevertheless, the power imbalance between employers and employees also results in underpayment or a complete lack of salary for an extended period of time. Many will put up with the employer's assurances, for some number of months, that they will be paid the full amount. Some eventually made claims after their employers stopped paying or as they realised that their promises will never come to fruition.

Many also face severe difficulties when seeking new employers. For those who did manage to find new employers, many would be broke as the lack of salaries caused them to exhaust their savings. This serves as a factor that reduces their ability to find new jobs due to the nature of employment agencies - they demand upfront payment of excessive agency fees⁴ before agreeing to place them with employers.

Lastly, even if migrant workers wanted to defend their rights, they face a lack of means to do so. There is extremely limited union representation for migrant workers in Singapore. A public demonstration, even if peaceful and organized, is banned for them,⁵ since they are not Singaporeans. Their unfamiliarity with the laws and policies as well as the governing system in Singapore puts them at an even greater disadvantage. Participants can study the possibilities of advocacy for migrant workers, to foster awareness amongst the public about the predicament of migrant workers, creating public pressure for better, stricter and more comprehensive labour laws. Other possibilities include collaborations with existing social groups that already specialise in providing migrant workers with legal aid and consultations.

Poor accommodation and living standards

The lacklustre living standards in foreign worker dormitories in Singapore, otherwise unseen by the masses, was recently brought to public attention due to the COVID situation. Foreign worker dormitories are cramped and enclosed, with many workers sharing the same room, packed into bunk beds. Currently, it is estimated that there are 200,000 migrant workers housed in 43 purpose-built dormitories around Singapore.⁶

Nevertheless, it is to be noted that there have been governmental measures to ensure the quality of living conditions of the migrant workers. Licensed dormitories housing more than 1,000 workers each are required to comply with the requirements under the Foreign Employee Dormitory Act (FEDA), such as providing facilities that include sick bays and isolation rooms.⁷

The effectiveness of these measures, especially when put to the test, is still in question. Taking the COVID period as an example, the cramped and stale conditions in the dormitories have contributed to the rapid spread of COVID in the migrant dorms causing large scale outbreaks. By the end of April 2020, the number of coronavirus cases in Singapore increased by 15 times, swelling from 1,000 to more than 15,000 cases. By May 2020, it had the largest number of cases of any country in Southeast Asia – with more than 90% of infections within the migrant workforce.⁸ Since the outbreaks amongst the migrant workers started, the migrant workers have been living under tight restrictions for more than 18 months, even as most of them are fully vaccinated. The separate treatment of these workers is evident; with Singapore striving to transit to a “new normal” and many looking forward to further easing of pandemic-related restrictions, the situation has not changed much for the 275,000-odd migrant workers living in dormitories.

Since last year, the workers have been mostly confined to their rooms due to the restrictions imposed, and only allowed to leave for work or, for stipulated recreation centres on some days. This has impacted their mental health negatively⁹. In view of this, as well as their poor living conditions, participants can consider possible actions that we can take to improve their situation. Community projects that participants can contemplate include meaningful engagement sessions with the migrant workers and raising more awareness about the plight of the migrant workers.

Other issues include questions about hygiene in the dormitories. Factory Converted Dormitories (“FCDs”) – a type of accommodation that is already regulated by the FEDA – are plagued by serious hygiene-related issues. During the 2021 Committee of Supply, it was revealed that stray animals were found to freely roam in certain FCD kitchens, in addition to unclean food preparation and disposal areas.¹⁰

Alienation of migrant workers

Despite the migrant workers comprising an essential part of Singapore’s workforce, stereotypes and prejudices exist against migrant workers whose jobs are seen to be 3D: Dirty, Dangerous and Demeaning. Such prejudices and biases have been exacerbated during the COVID pandemic due to the outbreak within the migrant workforce.¹¹

A 2019 study on public knowledge and attitudes towards migrant workers in Japan, Malaysia, Singapore, and Thailand by the International Labor Organization and UN Women reported that positive attitudes towards migrant workers have declined over the last decade, even as overall labour migration has increased. Majority of survey respondents in the four countries perceived that migrant workers increased their crime rates and threatened their country’s culture and heritage. Some among the public thought that migrant workers have a poor work ethic and that they cannot trust them.¹²

Locally, anti-migrant sentiments have also been published in mainstream media. Popular socialite Jamie Chua drew flak for an Instagram post about how she had a "disturbing nightmare" where she "dreamt of the Indian workers’ dorm and they were all rushing into my house".¹³ A forum letter published in Chinese daily Lianhe Zaobao blamed the Covid-19 outbreak in dormitories on migrant workers' personal hygiene and living habits. The writer made xenophobic claims on how migrant workers come from less developed countries and says that their "lifestyle habits" - such as resting under trees or on fields or gathering with their friends to drink beer and chat - would accompany them here.¹⁴

As for the solutions, integrating migrant workers into neighbourhoods, rather than isolating them in far-flung dormitories, is ideal long-term. The discussion has been made about integrating migrant worker dormitories into residential areas, to increase interaction between the general public and foreign workers, increasing physical integration There is, however, no current feasible plan yet, as locals demonstrate an unwillingness to let dormitories be built in residential areas. For instance, in 2008, Serangoon Gardens residents signed a petition objecting to a new foreign worker dormitory in their neighbourhood. Participants may ponder upon the possibilities of integrating the migrant worker community with the general population. How might community projects make a crucial contribution to establishing consistent and meaningful interactions between migrant workers and the general public?

Prominent Organisations

Healthserve¹⁵

HealthServe offers general medical services as well as dermatology, orthopaedic, occupational and physical therapy, and dental services. They provide casework and legal support to migrant workers with work injury claims and legal, criminal or employment-related issues. Each year, they assist on hundreds of cases. Their social workers follow up on their cases closely, for example, accompanying workers to the hospital for their checkups. Trained volunteer counsellors provide weekly mental and emotional support to these workers. Social assistance programs to unemployed migrant workers, including free meals, subsidized transport, an emergency shelter, and an emergency fund. They also organise events with the intention of building a community where the migrants feel supported and accepted and build skills such as English and computer skills for the migrant workers.

Transient workers count too (TWC2)¹⁶

One of the largest NGOs in Singapore, TWC2 provides advice and case follow up regarding cases of abuse, unfair work practices, tardy work payments or unreasonable demands from their employers. Programmes they run include Outreach Day, where volunteers visit places commonly visited by migrant workers to hand out fliers spreading awareness about their rights and options, or even answer any questions on the spot; the Cuff Road Project which offers a free breakfast and an ample dinner every weekday and lunch on Saturdays for unemployed migrant workers; and other advocacy efforts involving social media, newsletters and email, collaborations with media organisations and public and school talks.
