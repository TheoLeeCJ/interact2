Poverty

General Overview¹

Despite Singapore’s robust economic growth, evidence of poverty has also risen sharply in the same period. Although poverty in Singapore is not as severe as in developing countries, the lack of an official poverty line increases the difficulty for organisations to obtain data for better assessment of Singapore’s poor and for the poor to learn about available schemes. Overview²

While Singapore does provide financial aid, state support payouts are insufficient to cover the required amount for dignified living for many elderly recipients. With CPF and other annuities accounting for just 13% of elderly’s income and public assistance such as the Silver Support Scheme accounting for 2%³, there is strong evidence that state support is a severely insufficient income source.

Elderly

Overview²

Between 2012 and 2015, poverty levels among the aged increased by 74.32%. While Singapore does provide financial aid, state support payouts are insufficient to cover the required amount for dignified living for many elderly recipients. With CPF and other annuities accounting for just 13% of elderly’s income and public assistance such as the Silver Support Scheme accounting for 2%³, there is strong evidence that state support is a severely insufficient income source.

Key Issues

Firstly, Singapore strictly gatekeeps excessively targeted schemes with varying criteria attached⁴. This not only causes mass confusion amongst the elderly, worsened by their poor proficiency in English and technological illiteracy; but those in dire need of support also fall through the cracks. This can act as a disincentive to increase their earnings, giving rise to cases such as: “He says he’s been asking his boss to lower his pay – because he’s afraid he might get a reduced subsidy on his S$50-a-month rental flat”⁵.

“State support should be a safety net, not a means of last resort.”

Secondly, Singapore has no official statistics for elderly poverty⁶. The majority of data is gathered by non-governmental organisations (NGOs) such as the Lien Foundation and academics. Hence, such information is neither collated nor verified, making it difficult for NGOs, the government and researchers to understand the severity of the situation and provide adequate support.

“Researchers don’t understand the schemes fully, civil servants don’t often understand all the schemes available, so how would those people know where to get help?” - Dr Ng Kok Hoe, Lee Kuan Yew School of Public Policy

Lastly, the elderly’s outlook to state support also acts as an obstacle. The Merdeka generation’s mentality of independence crops up repeatedly, resulting in the working elderly often turning down support from family or social assistance even if they are already struggling financially. Furthermore, they rather be self-reliant and “work until cannot move”⁷, making them prone to sickness and injury. This not only further increases financial strain from medical fees, but also threatens their source of income as they often work manual jobs.

Evaluation of Policies and Measures

For food insecurity⁸, many elderly remain food insecure despite over a hundred food assistance groups providing aid. Firstly, the lack of standardisation and official information such as household distribution of poverty and a central database on charities for each area results in some households (especially seniors) being overserved while others are underserved. Such food assistance are also irregular as they are unable to function as a fully-fledged food charity, leaving households worrying about a reliable food source.

Next, the lack of feedback channels not only results in households receiving inappropriate foods for their nutritional needs or their physical limitations, bringing more health problems and food wastage. On the recipients' side, not wanting to seem ungrateful prevents them from providing valuable feedback voluntarily.

Lastly, food charities are restricted by the donations received, which often include unhealthy canned food or excessive non-perishables with a long shelf life.

“Holding up a can of fried dace fish with black beans, Fion sighed: “This is deep fried, it’s salty, it’s preserved. Aunty already has the three ‘highs’. Let me tell you frankly. If she eats all these, it is a slow suicide.”

Seeing that food insecurity is a more community-based problem, how can both the (i) general public (donors) and (ii) charities better support them? Consider partnerships with various stakeholders (government, businesses, schools etc) and how other countries approached these problems.

Current policies include Pioneer Generation Package⁹, Central Provision Fund Lifelong Income For the Elderly (CPF LIFE)¹⁰ and the Silver Support Scheme and additional support under ComCare¹¹.

Policies/Measures and its Purpose

Policies/Measures and its Purpose

  • Pioneer Generation Package:

    It helps offset healthcare costs for life and those eligible will automatically enjoy the subsidies.

  • CPF LIFE¹²:

    A national longevity insurance annuity scheme that automatically ensures all elderly born after 1985 will receive a monthly payout for as long as they live.

  • Silver Support Scheme:

    All Singaporeans aged 65 and above will be automatically assessed for their eligibility to receive Silver Support payouts. Payouts are given according to the type of HDB flat they live in and household monthly income per person.

  • Comcare Long-Term Assistance Scheme (Additional Support):

    Singaporeans aged 65 and above under ComCare Long-Term Assistance scheme receive additional $360 per quarter, medical assistance at public health centres and free or highly subsidised social support services.

“When you ask for ComCare assistance, they will check for everything… That’s why a lot of people don’t apply anymore after that. It’s so daunting, the whole process.” - Ms Wong Yock Leng, a senior social worker with Tsao Foundation¹³

Specifically, the Agency for Integrated Care has been designated as the central agency¹⁵ to coordinate social services and financial aid¹⁶ for seniors and caregivers under the Senior Cluster Network.¹⁷

First, analyse the current strengths (Overlapping policies for better support; Ease of application etc.) and weaknesses (Any excluded groups; Insufficient support etc.) of current state support.

Second, what are some on-the-ground solutions which citizens can initiate to fill the gaps of such policies? For example, are there ways for us to better connect the elderly to Family Service Centres and Social Service Offices?¹⁸

Single- Parent Families

Overview¹⁹

This study guide will define “single-parent households” as (i) divorcees with children, (ii) widowed with children, (iii) unwed parent and (iv) parent temporarily parenting alone due to incarceration, hospitalisation or other short-term absence of spouse or partner. Singstat recently released the marital status of all Singaporeans in July 2021²⁰, where 5.3% and 4.3% of residents aged over 15 years are widowed and divorced/separated²¹ respectively. Like many policies and assistance schemes, this does not take into account specifically the unwed parents nor temporary single parents, but it already indicates the significant proportion of non-nuclear families. Since most schemes support nuclear families, single parent households tend to fall through the gaps.

Key Issues

Some elements of financial support under the Marriage and Parenthood Package²² are designated only for “traditional” family units²³, excluding single-parent families from full entitlements²⁴ and differentiating between types of single parents²⁵. Even for long-term financial assistance available, the lack of access to information, tedious paperwork and long processing time are significant barriers.

There are 4 major issues regarding employment for single parents — skill inadequacy, work-family role conflicts and outstanding debt. Firstly, single parents with low-level education are unable to easily promote or even seek adequate employment due to time poverty and financial pressure. Hence they settle for low-paying jobs²⁶, earning less than the average monthly household income²⁷.

Even when employed, single parents may sacrifice stable, sufficient pay for workplace flexibility to balance childcare responsibilities. Furthermore, inadequate childcare makes seeking employment hugely challenging; yet some childcare subsidies require employment.

Finally, families tend to prioritise immediate needs over paying outstanding debt²⁸ and other bills, resulting in longer-term problems and perpetuation of poverty.

In the long run, the cycle of poverty also affects their children’s prospects. Unable to afford learning resources, children from low-income families may fall behind their peers. This affects their qualification level which is detrimental to employment prospects, ultimately limiting their income level and ability to progress financially.

Evaluation of Policies and Measures

It could be said that a significant number of family policies support nuclear households, and may not be beneficial or even inimical to single parents.

For Employment

To help single parents balance between work and family commitment, the state provides financial support – infant and childcare subsidies²⁹ and homecare support.

Policies/Measures and its Purpose

Policies/Measures and its Purpose

  • Child Care Financial Assistance:

    Further financial assistance if Child Care fees are above the Basic and Additional Subsidies and unaffordable such as a one-time grant to cover start-up costs of enrolling a child in the centre³⁰.

  • Levy Concessions for Migrant Domestic Workers³¹:

    As long as single adults live with a child below 16, they are eligible for a levy concession to hire a maid to help out around the house.

  • Tripartite Standards:

    Aims to increase the adoption of fair and progressive workplace practices aid single parent families, especially unwed parents³².

  • Working Mother’s Child Relief (WMCR)³³:

    Income tax relief for divorced or widowed working mothers.

Despite the nudge towards embracing a progressive workforce, there is still much room for improvement. How can we increase understanding and acceptance for single parents in the workforce? Even so, are there workplace considerations which prevent these disadvantages from being addressed? Finally, do consider effective solutions which communities can implement to increase understanding for single parents.

For Non-Financial Support

Under Ministry of Social and Family Development (MSF) newly launched Community Link (Comlink) initiative³⁴ aims to provide Comprehensive, Convenient and Coordinated (3C) support to empower families in rental housing. Firstly, Comlink Alliance, which is led by MSF’s Social Service Office (SSO) consists of various partner agencies and volunteers, will closely support each family with regular check-ins and customise support according to their needs. Secondly, Comlink Alliance brings together government agencies, the private sector and volunteers, collating different programmes benefiting both children and adults. It also acts as a platform to coordinate large-scale community-based government initiatives, including the ECDA’s KidSTART and MOE’s UPLIFT, facilitating movement of resources from different organisations (Schools, Corporates, Individuals).

How can such initiatives be better expanded to other areas — demographics (other single parents) and issues-wise (social support; mental health etc)? What are the possible limitations to such initiatives?

Similar to any other social issue, there is a lack of consolidated information and list of organisations providing different types of aid. Hence, single parents must comb through online articles and blog posts to find possible NGOs providing emotional and practical support or online social support groups³⁵, which is a time-consuming and draining process especially with the existing burden of becoming a single parent.

Should the government aid these support groups and if yes, how? Consider current local initiatives (eg. mental health) or case studies overseas.³⁶

Race

Overview

As of 2010, the proportion of each race in the citizen population is 76.2% Chinese, 15.0% Malay, 7.3% Indian and 1.3% “Others”.⁴⁶ One might expect the racial distribution for financial indicators to also be proportionate. However, at the high end of the income spectrum (monthly incomes of SGD 15,000), 11% of Singaporean Chinese and Indian headed households earn such income, compared to the 2% of Singaporean Malay headed households.⁴⁷

As such, there is evidence of income inequality between races, where the majority racial group in Singapore — Chinese — are on average wealthier than other racial groups.⁴⁸ Such income inequality can not only threaten the fragile racial harmony of Singapore but more importantly, it is the state duty to serve all citizens regardless of race to the best of their ability.

“We, the citizens of Singapore, pledge ourselves as one united people, regardless of race, language or religion, to build a democratic society based on justice and equality so as to achieve happiness, prosperity and progress for our nation."

Key Issues

Despite such discrepancies, there is a lack of race-based data, preventing relevant organisations from understanding which sectors require more support and how to bridge these gaps. However, race-based data may lead to divisiveness, especially against the backdrop of racism.⁴⁹ Since race-based data regarding poverty is not easily available, this study guide will focus on a few contentious race-related issues, namely: Chinese Privilege in Employment and Education, Race-based Self Help Groups and HDB’s Ethnic Integration Policy, and how they will affect income inequality or worsen poverty amongst racial minorities.

Seeing that racial harmony is a sensitive issue, do you think race-based data is beneficial overall? If yes, what are some community-based solutions that can ease tensions?

Chinese Privilege in Employment and Education

Chinese privilege⁵⁰ is defined as covert⁵¹ advantages enjoyed by the majority race in Singapore. There are 2 opposing views as to whether Chinese privilege exists.

Chinese Privilege does NOT exist Chinese Privilege EXISTS

Prime Minister Lee Hsien Loong supports that it does not, asserting that the Chinese have made concessions by accepting English as the lingua franca, equalising the playing field.⁵²

Employment requirements, although unfair, are seen as human nature to be drawn to those with similar cultural background⁵³ ⁵⁴. SAP schools are also an effort to support races in preserving their rich cultural heritage.

Racial minorities agree that there is some extent of Chinese privilege but may hinder open racial conversation when weaponised to throw back at aggressors⁵⁵. It also frames the majority race as the root problem, leading to finger-pointing which obscures true policy-making problems.

Examples include employers requiring Mandarin proficiency or workplace microaggressions to Special Assistance Plan (SAP) schools focused on Chinese culture simultaneously being academically elite schools.

Overall, the fact that it reduces the education and job prospects of minorities remains, giving the Chinese an edge. When covert racism is blindsided, it may eventually lead to systemic obstacles such as race will affecting hiring decisions, proven by a recent study⁵⁶. This has already led to racial minorities taking up lower-paying jobs, for example majority of Malay workers are concentrated in lower rung vocations prone to unemployment, especially during the pandemic.⁵⁷

“There’s some kind of privilege — that if you’re part of the majority, then certainly there are some things that make it a little easier for you”. — Dr Mathew Matthews, IPS Principal Researcher

Do you think Chinese privilege exists? If yes, should examples which support such privilege be abolished or adapted (eg. SAP schools)? If no, how do you propose we address such discrepancies not specifically targeted at any race, but arise from being a majority?

Race-based Self Help Groups

Secondly, race-based self-help groups were set up in such a way so as to encourage each ethnic group to be responsible for its own success, and to encourage self-reliance. These groups were founded on the belief that each racial group would be best aware of the problems through their community’s cultural and ethnic lens⁵⁸ and were best able to address them. The four self-help groups are the Chinese Development Assistance Council, the Singapore Indian Development Association (Sinda), Yayasan Mendaki and the Eurasian Association. As the funding from these groups primarily comes from their own communities via individual CPF donation, where the higher the income earned, the more they donate. However, this may continue to perpetuate inequality as communities in the lead can maintain that advantage by virtue of having more to give⁵⁹.

Do you think the government should work in tandem with these race-based self-help groups to prevent any one group from having an advantage? If yes, how should the government support them and how can we address possible tensions that may arise?

Ethnic Integration Policy

Lastly, the Ethnic Integration Policy (EIP) is an ethnic residential quota system set at block or neighbourhood levels of HDB towns based on the ethnic make-up of Singapore.⁶⁰ Since the EIP aims to promote racial integration and prevent racial enclaves, if Chinese residents in estates exceed the quota, ethnic minorities cannot sell to Chinese buyers who are the largest groups of potential buyers. Hence, these minorities will suffer from either a prolonged period without a suitable buyer or accept a lower selling price, which exacerbates the income inequality.⁶¹ Additionally, sale prices for racial group-constrained HDB resale units were different, where Chinese-constrained properties were more expensive than Indian or Malay-constrained properties, directly proving the discriminatory impact of EIP.⁶²

Should EIP still be implemented? If no, what are other possible measures to prevent ethnic enclaves? If yes, why is EIP still relevant?
