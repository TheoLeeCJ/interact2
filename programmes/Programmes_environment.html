Environment

General Overview

In general, Singapore has been doing relatively well in employing environmentally-friendly practices. Through the wide use of policies, support of environmental advocacy groups, and other governmental organisations that serve to steer Singapore in a sustainable route of development, Singapore has definitely obtained great results in its fight against climate change. However, there are a variety of issues that many Singaporeans overlook when considering the environmental problems in Singapore. These include food insecurity, especially amongst the poor, plastic waste, as well as threats to our biodiversity. Read on to find out more!

Key Issues

Food Insecurity

A pertinent issue that Singapore faces is food insecurity, defined as a lack of regular access to safe and nutritious food for normal growth and development and an active and healthy lifestyle by the Food and Agriculture Organisation (FAO).

Most Singaporeans have access to healthy and nutritious food. However, the impoverished in Singapore still faces the problem of food security issues. For example, 10% of Singaporeans struggle to get sufficient, safe and nutritious food, of which about 62% did not seek food support. Participants may wish to consider why there is a large proportion of households that are unwilling to seek food support.

In Singapore, limited resources exacerbated by land scarcity poses a serious problem to Singapore’s food security. In fact, only 1% of Singapore’s land use is available for food production and more than 90% of its food supply is imported. This entails that Singapore’s food source stability is highly vulnerable to global situations, meaning that Singaporeans may fall into states of food insecurity in times of global crises.

From a more optimistic perspective, there have been several current measures targeted at tackling this issue of food insecurity. For example, the general support for local farmers is increasing. Technological advancements allow urban farmers to maximise spatial usage, through vertical gardens and hydroponics. Participants can explore the following farms that have made use of such technology: Singapore Aquaculture Technologies’ Smart Floating Fish Farm (Fish - AI), Sky Greens (Plants - equipment), and N&N Agriculture (Eggs - equipment). Participants may wish to reflect on whether or not this encouraging trend can be a sustainable method of mitigation for Singapore’s food insecurity issue.

Apart from increasing local food production and encouraging the public to support local, Singapore has also provided gains and financial support for farmers in Singapore. For example between October 2014 to September 2020, a total of almost $42 million has been committed to 115 farms in Singapore. An example of this is the Agricultural Productivity Fund.

Lastly, Singapore has also been committed to diversifying its food sources. Currently, Singapore imports food from more than 170 countries.

Plastic Waste

Plastic is non-biodegradable and takes approximately 450 years to decompose. Improper plastic waste disposal has led to trash build up that severely degrades the environment. On the other hand, burning of plastic waste will result in toxic gases being released into the atmosphere, contributing to air pollution.

A main source of plastic waste is plastic bags, and the fast fashion industry. Notably, both plastic bags and the fast fashion industry are things that contribute to the convenience of people’s lives and their standards of living respectively. Hence, it is difficult for there to be a revolutionary change in this area, and is in fact unrealistic for consumers to give up these products. Participants should consider how we may encourage people to seek a balance between saving the earth and maintaining their current way of life.

Furthermore, the lack of recycling efforts worldwide also negatively contributes to plastic waste. In Singapore, the overall recycling rate in 2020 was at 52%, falling from 2019’s 59%. This is further exacerbated by the Covid-19 situation, where people are forced to stay home and online shopping deliveries, takeaway, food packages, food deliveries and many other forms of plastic waste generation contribute significantly to trash production. On the other hand, it has to be noted that trash and pollution contributed at workspaces have been greatly reduced.

Closer to home, Singapore is also facing the threats of trash build up. The Semakau Landfill, Singapore’s first and only off-shore landfill is expected to be filled up by 2035 - just 14 years from now - at our current rate of trash generation.

However, there has been work done to combat the increasing rate of trash generation, especially in Singapore. These include zero-waste grocery stores, straw-free initiatives, ground-up projects that take part in environmental advocacy as well as social enterprises such as Pulpit and Swapaholic.

Specific to the filling up of the Semakau Landfill, Singapore launched the Zero Waste Master Plan on 30 August 2019. The Masterplan hopes to reduce the waste sent to Semakau Landfill each day by 30% by 2030. Key points of the Master Plan includes establishing a circular economy approach - sustainable production, sustainable consumption and sustainable waste and resource management, as well as the Resource Sustainability Act, and the co-creating solutions with the community through the #RecycleRight campaign.

Threat to Biodiversity (including Animals)

Most of the main threats to species arise from habitat loss and degradation, pollution, poaching, and competition from alien species. Almost all of these arise from human action, whether directly or indirectly, especially alien species, which are those artificially introduced to the environment.

Some key threats to biodiversity are human activities, the loss of habitat for animals, deforestation, desertification, changes in the marine environment, increasing wildlife trading, and lastly, climate change. In Singapore specifically, development pressures and coastal modifications continue to be the main threats to the remaining intertidal habitats. For example, land reclamation along Singapore’s coast has decreased the coral reef cover by about 60%.

Aside from these, other threats such as climate change and ocean acidification are less defined or understood, but still have a very significant impact on biodiversity. A salient example of this is how high sea temperatures caused a mass coral bleaching event in Singapore, 1998.

Key Stakeholders

Government

Prior to the UNFCCC Climate Change Conference in Copenhagen in 2009, Singapore pledged to reduce greenhouse gas emissions to 16 per cent below business-as-usual (BAU) levels in 2020. In keeping with the 2015 Paris Climate Agreement, Singapore has made a further pledge to reduce emissions by 36 per cent by 2030 from 2005 levels, and stabilise our greenhouse gas emissions.

Green Plan 2030

The Singapore Green Plan 2030, or the Green Plan, is a whole-of-nation movement to advance Singapore’s national agenda on sustainable development. It aims to concretize ambitious plans over the next 10 years, and strengthen Singapore’s commitments under the UN’s 2030 Sustainable Development Agenda and Paris Agreement, thus allowing us to reach our long-term net zero emissions goal as soon as we can. Key targets include: Planting 1 million more trees, quadrupling solar energy deployment by 2025, reducing the waste sent to landfill by 30% by 2030, having at least 20% of schools to be carbon neutral by 2030, and ensuring that all newly registered cars use cleaner energy from 2030.

National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan

Singapore’s primary policy is to protect key indigenous ecosystems within our Nature Reserves, through the National Biodiversity Strategy and Action Plan.

There are currently 4 main plans to safeguard our biodiversity - these include implementing species conservation and recovery programmes; rehabilitating areas that have previously been degraded; extending green corridors to counter fragmentation; and utilising parks for ex-situ conservation and to house or re-create ecosystems that have been lost.

Social Enterprises or Movements

Non-Governmental Organisations and the people sector can contribute significantly to a greener future in Singapore. We suggest two examples that serve as a starting point for research - SG Clean, a social movement, and Green Nudge, a social enterprise.

SG Clean

SG Clean seeks to encourage the public and relevant stakeholders to play their roles in adopting not only good personal hygiene habits, but also to cultivate a sense of social responsibility. Overall, SG Clean aims to instill in Singaporeans a habit of keeping Singapore clean.

Green Nudge

Green Nudge originated as an idea when founder Li Seng wanted mass events to be more sustainable. He recognised that a business model balancing profit and impact could better shape long term sustainable behaviour, and thus formally set up Green Nudge as a company.

Green Nudge hopes to be the go-to sustainability business partner to build thriving green communities. Green Nudge’s strategy is to offer collaborations with companies, and through this carry out activities that promote environmental sustainability. Examples of their programmes are green activities like coastal cleanups, educational outreach involving learning journeys and talks, and also sustainability consultation, where they offer consults to businesses to show them how they can enhance their organisation’s environmental sustainability.

General Public

Climate change is a threat that people tend to overlook. Not only does it seem distant, but it is also perceived to affect other people instead of oneself. The widespread tendency to have optimism bias may also make it easier for people to assume such distant problems won’t affect them.

Regardless of this, everyone still has an important role to play in saving the environment. No matter how small the impact may be, we all have the ability to change the way we live, and this makes the change worthwhile. Recycling, reducing food waste, and saving water and electricity where possible are but a few ways that we can do so.

Key Questions

When thinking of possible ground-up solutions to environmental challenges that Singapore may face, do consider the perspectives of various stakeholders.

General Public

  1. How willing are people to make fundamental changes in their way of living to save the environment?
  2. How will people’s lives be impacted by environmental policies implemented by the government?
  3. Is it really possible to change people’s mindsets and habits to become more eco-friendly? If so, how can we do so? If not, what else can be done?
